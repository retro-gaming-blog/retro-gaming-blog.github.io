Unlock the Nostalgia: Your Ultimate Guide to Retro Gaming Handhelds in 2023

Ah, 2023. A year of flying cars, robot butlers, and... Game Boys? That's right, folks. In this era of VR headsets and 4K gaming, we're going to take a step back. Way back. Back to a time when pixels were bigger than your thumb, and the only "cloud" was the one blocking your game because you were playing outside. (Remember outside? Yeah, me neither.)

A Brief History of Time... and Handhelds

Let's start from the beginning. And by beginning, I mean the 1970s. For those of you who don't know, that's when dinosaurs roamed the earth and the first handheld games appeared. They were simple LED-based games, like Mattel's Auto Race. But hey, we all have to start somewhere, right?

Fast forward to the 1980s, and we got the Game Boy. This little brick of a machine was the first to bring gaming into the palms of our sweaty little hands. And boy, did we love it. Tetris, anyone?

The Classics

Speaking of Game Boy, let's talk about the classics. You know, those games that you could play for hours on end, even though they were just a bunch of pixels on a screen. Tetris, Super Mario Land, The Legend of Zelda: Link’s Awakening… these were the games that defined a generation. And by generation, I mean those of us old enough to remember when the internet made that awful dial-up noise.

But the Game Boy wasn't the only player in the game (pun totally intended). There was also the Sega Game Gear, the Atari Lynx, and the NEC TurboExpress. These were the heavy hitters of the handheld world, each with their own library of games and unique features. The Game Gear had a color screen (wow!), the Lynx had ambidextrous controls (cool!), and the TurboExpress could play the same games as its home console counterpart (amazing!).

The New Kids on the Block

Fast forward to 2023, and retro gaming handhelds are back in a big way. We've got companies like Analogue and Hyperkin creating new devices that play old games, and they're doing it with style. These aren't your dad's Game Boys, folks. They've got high-res screens, rechargeable batteries, and they can play games from multiple systems. It's like having a whole arcade in your pocket!

And then there's the DIY scene. With a Raspberry Pi and a little bit of elbow grease, you can create your own handheld gaming system. And the best part? You can load it up with all your favorite games from yesteryear. It's like a time machine, but for gaming.

How to Get Your Hands on One

So, you're sold on the idea of retro gaming handhelds. But how do you get your hands on one? Well, you've got a few options. You could scour eBay for an original Game Boy or Game Gear, but be prepared to pay a pretty penny. These things are collectors' items now, after all.

Your other option is to go for one of the new devices from Analogue or Hyperkin. They're a bit pricier than your average handheld, but they're worth it for the quality and compatibility.

And of course, there's always the DIY route. If you're handy with a soldering iron and don't mind a bit of coding, you can build your own system. There are plenty of guides online to help you get started.

The Future of Retro Gaming

So, where do we go from here? Well, I don't have a crystal ball, but I can make a few educated guesses.

Firstly, I think we'll continue to see new devices that play old games. There's a market for nostalgia, and companies like Analogue and Hyperkin are tapping into it.

Secondly, I think we'll see more and more people getting into the DIY scene. It's never been easier to build your own system, and the community is incredibly supportive.

And finally, I think we'll see more and more games being re-released on modern platforms. We've already seen this with the Nintendo Switch Online service, and I think it's only going to increase.

So there you have it, folks. Your ultimate guide to retro gaming handhelds in 2023. Whether you're a seasoned gamer or a newbie, there's never been a better time to get into retro gaming. Now if you'll excuse me, I've got a date with Super Mario Land.
