Top 10 Retro Gaming Handhelds: Uncover the Classics that Revolutionized Portable Gaming

Ah, retro gaming handhelds. Those little plastic rectangles of fun. Remember when playing a game meant more than just tapping on a shiny glass screen? When "portable gaming" didn't involve firing up an app on your smartphone but required you to pack an extra set of batteries? Let's indulge in a spot of nostalgia while we go back in time to when sprites had edges, screens had curves, and the, oh so sweet, bleeps and bloops of 8-bit music filled the air. Buckle up, grab a snack, and join me on this blocky journey through the top ten retro gaming handhelds that revolutionized the world of portable gaming!

1. Game Boy

Well, well, well, where do you think we would start?! The Game Boy by Nintendo, released in 1989, practically owned the '90s and for good reason. This game-changer with its pea-green and black screen, crunchy buttons, and bulky design might make today's gamers scoff, but in its heyday? This bad boy was 'portable gaming'. Tetris, anyone?

2. Game Gear

The Sega Game Gear might not have been as popular as the Game Boy, but it deserves its flowers for bringing backlit, colored gaming to the world of handhelds in 1990. Sure, it chomped through six AA batteries in a couple of hours (did Energizer and Duracell secretly fund it?), but when you had Sonic zipping across the screen in full color glory? Worth it.

3. Game Boy Advance

Oh, you thought we were done with Nintendo? Cute, you. The Game Boy Advance, debuting in 2001, was a heck of an upgrade from its predecessors. Sleek, colorful, and comfortable to use, it brought the Super Nintendo experience into our pockets. Remember squinting at the screen of your non-backlit GBA, trying to play 'Mario Kart: Super Circuit'? Ah, good times.

4. Atari Lynx

A bit of a dark horse, the Atari Lynx holds the title of the first handheld with a color LCD screen when it came out in 1989. Plus, it had the audacity to cater to left-handed people by offering ‘lefty’ mode. Revolutionary! Did it make a significant dent in the handheld market? Not quite. But is it remembered? Absolutely.

5. Neo Geo Pocket Color

In the Game Boy-dominated world of the late '90s, the Neo Geo Pocket Color dared to be different. Short lived as its existence was, this charmer featured a nifty tiny joystick and came with a suite of fantastic fighting games (shout-out to the 'King of Fighters'). A tragic case of being the right console at the wrong time.

6. PSP (PlayStation Portable)

In 2004, Sony entered the handheld arena with the PSP, a true behemoth. With a wide 4.3-inch screen, it made portable gaming feel almost like a home-console experience. Oh, the joy of playing 'Grand Theft Auto: Liberty City Stories' on the go! The PSP was a portable game-changer in every sense of the word.

7. Nintendo DS

Twin screens, touch controls, a clamshell design? This wasn't just a gaming console; it was a statement. The Nintendo DS was the quirky cousin that everyone loved when it launched in 2004. With games like 'Nintendogs' and 'Brain Age,' it even made non-gamers say, “Okay, maybe just one round.” A truly inclusive revolution in the palm of our hands.

8. WonderSwan

Oh, the WonderSwan! How many of you remember this one? Released only in Japan in 1999 by Bandai (Yeah, the 'Tamagotchi' folks), this little gem had a nice design, good graphics, and a library that made Japanese RPG fans salivate. What? You didn't have one? Well, that's your loss, my friend.

9. Sega Nomad

The Sega Nomad, launched in 1995, was essentially a portable Sega Genesis. It sucked batteries dry faster than you can say 'Sonic the Hedgehog,' but who cared when you could play your Genesis games wherever you wanted? Unfortunately, it ended up being pretty nomadic itself, wandering into the annals of gaming history, barely noticed.

10. TurboExpress

Lastly, we have the TurboExpress, an ambitious beast from 1990 by NEC that was a literal handheld version of the TurboGrafx-16 home console. It had a great screen (for the time) and could do something mind-boggling – play the same game cards as its big brother home console. Talk about a revolution! It was very expensive and so battery-hungry it made Game Gear look like a saint, but it was worth every penny to the lucky few who possessed it.

So, there you have it, the top ten retro handhelds that made us who we are, gamers with a heaping side of nostalgia. They may not hold a candle to your smartphone in terms of pixels or processing power, but boy do they have heart! And isn't that what gaming is all about? Well, that and thrashing your friends at 'Mario Kart'. Obviously.
