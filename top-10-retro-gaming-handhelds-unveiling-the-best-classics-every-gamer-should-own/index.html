Top 10 Retro Gaming Handhelds: Unveiling the Best Classics Every Gamer Should Own

Hey there, retro gaming enthusiasts! Are you ready for a wild ride back to the days of 8-bit graphics, chunky plastic consoles, and the sound of tiny speakers blaring out chiptune music? Of course, you are! So, buckle up, grab a pack of AA batteries (you'll understand why in a minute), and let's dive into the world of retro gaming handhelds!

1. Game Boy

Okay, let's get the obvious one out of the way. If you're a fan of retro handheld gaming, you probably already own a Game Boy (or three). Released in 1989, this little grey brick with its pea-green screen was a game-changer, quite literally. Who didn't love squinting at that tiny screen for hours, battling pixelated monsters in Pok√©mon Red and Blue or saving Princess Daisy in Super Mario Land? And remember the "battery low" light? That little red terror was the bane of many a long car journey!

2. Game Boy Color

The Game Boy Color, or GBC as we in the know like to call it, was the technicolor dreamcoat of handheld gaming. Released in 1998, it brought our favorite games to life in vibrant color. No more squinting at monochrome screens, now we had glorious 8-bit color! And who could forget the sheer joy of playing The Legend of Zelda: Oracle of Seasons in full color for the first time?

3. Game Boy Advance

Continuing the Game Boy love fest, let's not forget the Game Boy Advance. This sleek little number was the last in the Game Boy line and, boy, did it go out with a bang. With its wider screen and shoulder buttons, it felt like you had a real miniature console in your hands. And with games like Metroid Fusion and Advance Wars, it was a real powerhouse of gaming fun.

4. Sega Game Gear

Now let's switch gears (pun absolutely intended!) and talk about the Sega Game Gear. Released in 1990 as a competitor to the Game Boy, the Game Gear was like the flashy new kid on the block. It had a backlit color screen (take that, Game Boy!) and a wide game library. The only downside? This baby was a battery guzzler. Six AA batteries for only a few hours of play? Ouch!

5. Nintendo DS

Next up, we have the Nintendo DS. With its dual screens and touchscreen functionality, the DS was a real game-changer when it launched in 2004. Who didn't love the thrill of closing the clamshell after a successful game of Brain Age or Nintendogs? And let's not forget the sheer joy of PictoChat!

6. Sony PSP

The Sony PSP was Sony's attempt to muscle into the handheld market, and it came with some serious muscle! It had a gorgeous widescreen display and the ability to play movies and music. It was like having a mini PlayStation in your pocket. Remember the first time you fired up Grand Theft Auto: Liberty City Stories on the PSP? Pure gaming bliss.

7. Neo Geo Pocket Color

The Neo Geo Pocket Color, or NGPC for short, was a bit of a hidden gem in the world of retro handheld gaming. It had a great selection of games, including some excellent fighters like SNK vs. Capcom: The Match of the Millennium. Plus, it had a clicky thumbstick, which is just plain fun!

8. Nintendo Game & Watch

The Game & Watch series was Nintendo's first foray into handheld gaming and, let's be honest, they were more like fancy calculators than actual gaming consoles. But, hey, who didn't love playing Ball and Chef while waiting for the school bus?

9. Atari Lynx

The Atari Lynx was another competitor in the handheld market, and it had some unique features like a backlit color screen and the ability to flip the controls for left-handed players. But with a high price tag and a lack of good games, it never really took off. Still, it's a cool piece of gaming history!

10. TurboExpress

Last but not least, we have the TurboExpress. This handheld console was basically just a portable version of the TurboGrafx-16 home console. It was expensive and had a tiny screen, but it was also the only way you could play Bonk's Adventure on the go, so it's got that going for it!

So, there you have it, folks, the top 10 retro gaming handhelds that every gamer should own. Sure, they might not have the flashy graphics or online capabilities of today's handhelds, but they have something even better: nostalgia. And isn't that what retro gaming is all about?
